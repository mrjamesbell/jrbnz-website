# A Doll's House — production notes

Amy Herzog's 2023 adaptation strips the play back to something domestic and suffocating in the best way. Where older translations can feel formal or distanced, Herzog's language lands in the room with you — contemporary without being casual, precise without being cold.

We opened in August 2025 at the Loft Theatre after eight weeks of rehearsal. It won Best Direction at the Auckland Theatre Awards, which I still find slightly surreal.

## The adaptation

Herzog makes one structurally significant change: she allows Nora far more knowledge of her own situation than Ibsen does. The original Nora spends most of the play in a kind of wilful innocence — she knows what she has done, but not what it means. Herzog's Nora knows exactly what it means from early in Act One. That changes everything about how you play her relationship with Torvald.

The risk is that it removes some of the dramatic irony audiences expect. The reward is that it makes Nora's compliance in the first two acts a conscious, strategic choice — which is more interesting and more frightening.

    <h4>What Torvald actually believes</h4>
    <p>He's not a villain. That's the trap. He genuinely believes everything he says about love and protection and propriety. Directing him required finding the warmth inside the condescension — and then making that warmth the problem.</p>

## The ending

The door slam is one of the most famous stage directions in Western drama. The temptation is to play it as triumph or at least as release. We played it as grief — Nora leaving a life that, on some level, she had loved. Not an exit of liberation but of necessity.

Whether that's what Ibsen intended, I have no idea. It's what the production needed.
