# Twenty years of Shakespeare in the Park

The first one was *Much Ado About Nothing* in 2007. I'd been to the park a handful of times as an audience member and thought I understood what it required. I did not. Open-air Shakespeare requires a completely different relationship between text and space — larger physically, but also more exposed. There's nowhere to hide a bad scene.

Since then I've directed 17 more productions there, across 13 of Shakespeare's plays. Several of them I've returned to: *Hamlet* twice, *Measure for Measure* twice, *Much Ado About Nothing* twice, *Romeo and Juliet* twice. Revisiting a play from a different point in your own life turns out to be one of the more interesting things you can do as a director.

## What the park teaches you

You learn to trust the text because you have no other choice. Interior lighting, sound design, the architecture of an enclosed space — none of those are available to you in the same way. What you have is the language and the bodies delivering it.

This has made me a better director indoors too. I became much more suspicious of mood-setting that substitutes for acting, and much more interested in what happens when performers simply speak the words clearly and let them do their work.

## What I'd tell myself in 2007

Keep the interval shorter. Don't cast against type just to be interesting — cast against type because the actor makes you see the character differently. And trust that the audience will follow you into difficult territory if you commit fully to where you're going.

The park will always have its own ideas about what a production should look like: a plane overhead, someone's dog, the light changing. Learn to use what the space gives you instead of fighting it.
